About the Event

The FOC⁴S conference brings together early-career researchers working at the forefront of computational chemistry, electronic structure theory, classical and quantum-based simulations, as well as relevant computational methods, united by a common goal: to push the boundaries of modern theoretical and computational chemistry. The scope spans topics such as strongly correlated systems, excited states, density functional theory, classical force fields, molecular dynamics, and multiscale modeling, with applications ranging from single molecules to complex biological and solid-state systems.

Unlike traditional conferences, this meeting is not focused on incremental research updates, but on promoting in-depth discussions on broader challenges, emerging ideas, and conceptual advances relevant to the conference topics. The emphasis is on open dialogue, critical thinking, and collaborative exploration, where participants can brainstorm and collectively examine potential solutions and future directions. Speakers are strongly encouraged to present their topics in a clear and accessible manner, making them approachable to a broad audience and enabling meaningful feedback from all participants. The conference aims to create a supportive and safe environment for sharing new ideas and refining concepts through discussions with the wider community.

By promoting open exchange and interdisciplinary dialogue, the meeting intends to support the development of stronger and more competitive research proposals, inspire innovative research directions, and stimulate new collaborations. While the central focus remains on scientific exchange, we welcome discussions on teaching, communication, and evaluation of science.

Conference Board

Vladislav Slama

Charles University

vladislav.slama@matfyz.cuni.cz

Vladislav Slama received his PhD in Physics from Charles University in Prague under the supervision of Tomáš Mančal, with a primary focus on open quantum systems and the combination of stochastic approaches for optical spectra and exciton dynamics simulations with quantum chemical methods. He subsequently pursued postdoctoral research with Benedetta Mennucci at the University of Pisa, focusing on QM/MMPol polarizable embedding methods for excited states, with applications to biological light-harvesting complexes. In 2023, he joined the group of Ursula Rothlisberger at EPFL, where he worked on perovskite solar cells and molecular electronics, with a particular emphasis on donor–acceptor systems.
Since 2026, he has been an independent researcher at Charles University in Prague. His research focuses on light–matter interactions and multiexciton effects, ranging from electronic structure methods to the simulation of optical spectra.
At the conference, he is responsible for topics related to electronic excited states of molecular systems, electron transfer, and spectroscopy, and he serves as the organizing host for the 2026 edition.

Virginia Carnevali

EPFL

virginia.carnevali@epfl.ch

Virginia Carnevali graduated with a Master's degree in Physics from the University of Milan and completed her PhD in Physics at the University of Trieste. She then joined Central Michigan University for a PostDoc, before joining the group of Ursula Rothlisberger at EPFL in 2022 for a second PostDoc. Her research combines first-principles simulations, multiscale modeling, and high-performance computing to investigate structural, electronic, magnetic, and optical properties of materials. In particular, she develops and applies multiscale simulation strategies to design and optimize sustainable energy devices, with a focus on hybrid perovskite solar cells and thermoelectric systems. From September 2026, she will join the Physics Department of the University of Trieste and start her own research group. At the conference, she is responsible for topics related to condensed matter simulations and materials modeling.

Andrea Levy

EPFL

andrea.levy@epfl.ch

Andrea Levy graduated in Physics from the University of Milano-Bicocca, where his Master's thesis in the group of Cristiana Di Valentin focused on classical molecular dynamics simulations of inorganic nanoparticles as drug carriers. He then joined the group of Ursula Rothlisberger at EPFL to pursue a PhD in Computational Chemistry. His research focused on transition-metal-based compounds in biomolecular systems through multiscale method development within the MiMiC framework, predictive tools for metal binding site identification, and QM/MM molecular dynamics simulations. Since 2026, he has been a postdoctoral researcher in the same group, focusing on the development and advancement of multiscale simulation techniques, including polarizable embedding QM/MM. At the conference, he is responsible for topics related to method development and multiscale modeling.

Sophie Johnson

EPFL

sophia.johnson@epfl.ch

Sophia Johnson graduated with a Bachelor of Science in Chemistry from the University of Texas at Austin in 2012. She worked for some time at the same institute in curriculum development and program administration with a short break to work in information architecture at Indeed. In 2021, she joined the group of Ursula Rothlisberger at EPFL. Sophia studies the formation and detection of DNA damage caused by reactive oxygen species or interactions with metabolites of endocrine-disrupting chemicals (EDCs) via multiscale computational techniques, including hybrid QM/MM simulations. In 2024, she co-founded the EPFL association Science Policy Action Network (SPAN) to raise awareness of scientists' roles in policy for society or science, highlight relevant careers and organizations, train on transferable skills, and learn to generate policy recommendations. At the conference, she is responsible for topics related to biological systems and conversations on policy for science or science for policy.
